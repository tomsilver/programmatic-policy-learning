You will be given several continuous-control demonstration trajectories.
Observations are continuous-valued state vectors and actions are continuous-valued control vectors.
The demonstrations are serialized in a compact relational format to make state structure, object relationships, and action effects easier to read.

Observation / action field guide:
${OBSERVATION_FIELD_GUIDE}

Important interpretation notes:

- For the circular robot, `robot_x` and `robot_y` give the CENTER of the robot base.
- For the hook, `hook_x` and `hook_y` describe its anchored reference position, and `hook_theta` gives its orientation.
- The hook geometry is described by `hook_width`, `hook_length_side1`, and `hook_length_side2`.
- For circular buttons, position fields give the CENTER of the button and `radius` gives its size.
- `robot_base_radius` is the radius of the robot base. This matters when reasoning about reach, contact, and whether the robot can get close enough to the hook.
- Action dimensions represent the commanded control for one step:
  - `dx`, `dy`: translation
  - `dtheta`: rotation
  - `darm`: arm joint change
  - `vac`: vacuum/gripper control

Each trajectory is shown as a sequence of `*** Step t ***` blocks.

Each step may contain:

- `Observation (s_t):`
  A partial listing of important raw observation entries and their values at that step.

- `Action:`
  The action taken at that step.

- `CHANGES SUMMARY:`
  A concise description of which observation entries changed from `s_t` to `s_{t+1}`.

- `Relational Facts:`
  Human-readable geometric summaries derived from the raw observation, such as:
  - relative position or orientation,
  - distance, offset, contact, reachability, or alignment relations,
  - whether the robot is positioned to approach or use the hook,
  - whether the hook is close enough to influence the movable button,
  - whether the movable button is getting closer to the target button,
  - whether an action appears to move toward, away from, rotate toward, extend toward, push, or pull relative to task-relevant entities.

Important:
- These relational facts are derived summaries intended to help interpret the state.
- They are not additional input variables.
- Any feature code must use only the raw arrays `s` and `a`, indexed according to the field guide.

Geometric interpretation:
- Do not assume a fixed object ontology beyond what is supported by the field guide and demonstrations.
- Do not assume any particular coordinate convention unless it is specified in the field guide or implied by the trajectory summaries.
- Derived structures such as contact zones, reachability margins, approach directions, push/pull configurations, or interaction zones may not be directly stored variables; they may need to be inferred from raw state variables.
- The derived summaries are meant to expose reusable structure in the demonstrations, not episode-specific labels.

Task interpretation:
- Infer success-relevant behavior only from the field guide, state transitions, and demonstration patterns.
- Use the demonstrations to understand how actions relate to the hook, the movable button, the target button, contact opportunities, and progress toward pressing the target.
- Prefer reusable patterns that depend on structured relationships rather than absolute coordinates tied to one trajectory.

When proposing features, prefer predicates that capture reusable state-action structure:
- relationships between the robot, the hook, the movable button, the target button, and task-relevant interaction structure,
  - whether an action is appropriate given the current configuration,
  - progress, alignment, reach, contact, consistency, or constraint-respecting behavior,
  - conditional predicates that become relevant in different local regimes of the state space.

Additional guidance on feature quality:
- Prefer features that help explain why an action is appropriate in the current state, rather than features that only describe the state or only describe the action in isolation.
- Prefer reusable relational state-action predicates that could help distinguish expert actions from plausible alternative actions in similar states.
- Avoid features that are nearly constant across the demonstrations, overly specific to a single trajectory, or tied to control dimensions that do not appear behaviorally relevant in the demonstrations.
- Avoid generating many minor threshold variations of the same predicate unless they reflect meaningfully different behavioral regimes.
- Pay special attention to whether a predicate helps explain acquiring the hook, maneuvering the hook toward the movable button, or moving the movable button toward the target button.

Do not treat the textual summaries as privileged symbols.
Use them only to understand the meaning of the raw observation fields.
Any returned feature must be expressible directly from `s` and `a`.
