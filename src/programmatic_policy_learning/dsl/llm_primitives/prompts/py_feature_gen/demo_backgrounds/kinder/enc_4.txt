You will be given several continuous KinDER trajectories.

Observations are continuous-valued state vectors and actions are continuous control vectors.
The demonstrations are serialized in a compact relational format to make the geometry and action effects easier to read.

Observation / action field guide:
${OBSERVATION_FIELD_GUIDE}

Important interpretation notes:

- For the circular robot, `robot_x` and `robot_y` give the CENTER of the robot base.
- For rectangular objects such as the target and obstacles, `(x, y)` gives the BOTTOM-LEFT corner of the rectangle.
- Width and height fields describe rectangle extent from that bottom-left corner: width extends rightward and height extends upward.
- `robot_base_radius` is the radius of the robot base. This matters when reasoning about collision, clearance, and whether the robot can fit through narrow openings.
- Action dimensions represent the commanded control for one step:
  - `dx`, `dy`: translation
  - `dtheta`: rotation
  - `darm`: arm joint change
  - `vac`: vacuum/gripper control

Each trajectory is shown as a sequence of `*** Step t ***` blocks.

Each step may contain:

- `Observation (s_t):`
  A partial listing of important raw observation entries and their values at that step.

- `Action:`
  The action taken at that step.

- `CHANGES SUMMARY:`
  A concise description of which observation entries changed from `s_t` to `s_{t+1}`.

- `Relational Facts:`
  Human-readable geometric summaries derived from the raw observation, such as:
  - relative position (`left of`, `right of`, `above`, `below`)
  - distance or offset relations
  - whether the robot is inside a region
  - whether the robot is aligned with a corridor, opening, or gap
  - whether the robot is before, inside, or beyond a barrier-like structure

Important:
- These relational facts are derived summaries intended to help interpret the state.
- They are not additional input variables.
- Any feature code must use only the raw arrays `s` and `a`, indexed according to the field guide.

Geometric interpretation:
- For rectangular objects in this environment, including the target and obstacles, `(x, y)` denotes the BOTTOM-LEFT corner, not the center.
- Rectangle centers must be computed as `(x + width / 2, y + height / 2)`.
- For the circular robot, `(robot_x, robot_y)` denotes the CENTER of the robot base.
- A passage or gap is not a directly stored variable; it is a geometric relation inferred from obstacle positions and sizes.
- Statements such as “robot is left of passage wall” or “robot y is aligned with passage gap” are derived from obstacle geometry plus robot radius.
- These derived facts are meant to expose reusable spatial structure, not episode-specific labels.

Success condition:
- Episode termination occurs when the robot CENTER lies inside the target rectangle.
- Do not assume the entire circular robot must fit inside the target region for success.

What the demonstrations are meant to show:
- how the robot moves relative to objects and free space,
- how actions change position and orientation,
- how the robot’s behavior depends on geometric relationships rather than absolute coordinates.

When proposing features, prefer predicates that capture reusable state-action structure:
- relative geometry between robot, target, and obstacles,
- whether an action is appropriate given the current spatial configuration,
- progress, alignment, clearance, and constraint-respecting behavior,
- conditional features that depend on the current geometric regime.

Do not treat the textual summaries as privileged symbols.
Use them only to understand the meaning of the raw observation fields.
Any returned feature must be expressible directly from `s` and `a`.
